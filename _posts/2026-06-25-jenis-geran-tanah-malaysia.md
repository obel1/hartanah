Before you buy any property in Malaysia, the land title (geran) tells you what you actually own. Two things are worth checking on any title: the tenure, and whether there is a restriction in interest.

## Freehold vs leasehold

**Freehold (pegangan kekal)** means you own the property indefinitely. On the title, look for the words *"Pegangan untuk selama-lamanya"*, holding in perpetuity. Freehold titles are typically issued as *Geran* or *Geran Mukim*.

**Leasehold (pajakan)** means the state owns the land and you hold it for a fixed term, commonly 99 years, sometimes 60 or 30. The title will read *Pajakan Negeri* or *Pajakan Mukim* and state an expiry date. When the lease runs low, banks get stricter about financing (many hesitate below 60 years remaining), resale value softens, and renewing the lease requires an application to the state and payment of a premium.

## Sekatan kepentingan

Some titles carry a **Sekatan Kepentingan**, a restriction in interest. The standard wording is:

> Tanah ini tidak boleh dipindahmilik, digadai atau dipajak melainkan dengan kebenaran Pihak Berkuasa Negeri.

In plain terms: the property cannot be sold, charged (mortgaged), or leased without the **consent of the State Authority**. Every transaction gets an extra step, a consent application to the state, which adds time and carries a possibility of rejection.

In practice, this restriction is often used to enforce racial quota policy: if the current owner is Bumiputera, consent to transfer to a non-Bumiputera buyer may be refused. The approval criteria are not published. Decisions are made case by case inside the state authority, so there is no way to know in advance whether a given transfer will be approved.

## Leasehold does not always mean sekatan

Most people assume the two go together: leasehold has restrictions, freehold does not. Reality is messier.

**Some leaseholds have no sekatan at all.** Aster Residence in Cheras, for example, is leasehold, but the titles carry no restriction in interest. Transfers proceed without state consent.

**Some freeholds do carry sekatan.** Titles in Cyberjaya, Putrajaya, and parts of Johor (at least in what I have seen) are freehold on paper yet come with a restriction in interest requiring state consent for any dealing.

Tenure and restriction are two separate questions. Freehold tells you how long you own it. Sekatan tells you how freely you can deal with it. Both are answered by the specific title, not the marketing brochure.

**Pegangan** (page 1): it reads *"Pegangan untuk selama-lamanya"* under a *Geran Mukim*, which means freehold.

**Syarat Nyata** (page 2): the express condition, what the land may be used for. This one says *Pangsapuri Suite*: residential suite use.

**Sekatan Kepentingan** (page 2): this one says *Tiada*, meaning none. On other titles, this section may instead carry the restriction above, in which case any dealing requires State Authority consent.

## Checking a title

If you need to look up a title, official land searches are available through each state's Pejabat Tanah dan Galian (PTG) or e-Tanah portal. An online search usually costs around RM30.
