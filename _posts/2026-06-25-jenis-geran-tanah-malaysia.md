Before you buy any property in Malaysia, one document tells you what you're actually getting: the land title — the geran. Agents will summarise it for you. Lawyers will bill you to read it. But the two things that matter most take five minutes to check yourself, and in this post I'll show you exactly where to look — on my own geran.

![Page 1 of a Malaysian strata title (hakmilik strata), with private details redacted]({{ '/assets/jenis-geran/geran-muka1.jpg' | relative_url }})

## Freehold vs leasehold: the basics

**Freehold (pegangan kekal)** means you own the property indefinitely. On the title, look for the words *"Pegangan untuk selama-lamanya"* — holding in perpetuity. Freehold titles are typically issued as *Geran* or *Geran Mukim*.

**Leasehold (pajakan)** means the state owns the land and you hold it for a fixed term — commonly 99 years, sometimes 60 or 30. The title will read *Pajakan Negeri* or *Pajakan Mukim* and state an expiry date. When the lease runs low, three things happen: banks get stricter about financing (many hesitate below 60 years remaining), resale value softens, and eventually you must apply to renew the lease and pay a premium to the state — a process that can take months and is not automatic.

That's the textbook version. Here's the part the textbook doesn't tell you.

## The sekatan trap

Some titles carry a **Sekatan Kepentingan** — a restriction in interest. The standard wording is:

> Tanah ini tidak boleh dipindahmilik, digadai atau dipajak melainkan dengan kebenaran Pihak Berkuasa Negeri.

In plain terms: the property cannot be sold, charged (mortgaged), or leased without the **consent of the State Authority**. Every transaction gets an extra step — a consent application to the state — which adds weeks or months, and carries a real possibility of rejection.

In practice, this restriction is most often used to enforce racial quota policy: if the current owner is Bumiputera, consent to transfer to a non-Bumiputera buyer may simply be refused. But here is the uncomfortable truth — **it's a black box**. The approval criteria are not published anywhere. Decisions are made case by case inside the state authority, and nobody outside can tell you with certainty whether a given transfer will be approved. If you're buying a property with sekatan, price that uncertainty in — in time, in legal fees, and in the risk the deal collapses at the consent stage.

## Leasehold ≠ sekatan, freehold ≠ freedom

Most people assume the two go together: leasehold has restrictions, freehold doesn't. Reality is messier.

**Some leaseholds have no sekatan at all.** Aster Residence in Cheras, for example, is leasehold — but the titles carry no restriction in interest. Transfers proceed like any freehold deal, no state consent needed.

**Some freeholds DO carry sekatan.** Titles in parts of Cyberjaya, Putrajaya, and Johor are freehold on paper yet come with a restriction in interest requiring state consent for any dealing.

The lesson: **tenure and restriction are two separate questions.** Freehold tells you how long you own it. Sekatan tells you how freely you can deal with it. You must check both — and the answer depends on the specific title, not the marketing brochure.

## Where to look: a real example

This is page 2 of my own geran — a strata title (hakmilik strata) for a unit in Kuala Lumpur, with the private details blacked out:

![Page 2 of the same strata title showing Syarat Nyata and Sekatan Kepentingan sections]({{ '/assets/jenis-geran/geran-muka2.jpg' | relative_url }})

Three sections matter:

**Pegangan** (page 1): mine reads *"Pegangan untuk selama-lamanya"* under a *Geran Mukim* — freehold.

**Syarat Nyata** (page 2): the express condition — what the land may be used for. Mine says *Pangsapuri Suite*: residential suite use.

**Sekatan Kepentingan** (page 2): mine says one beautiful word — ***Tiada***. None. No state consent needed to sell, charge, or lease. When you're reviewing a title, this single line is what you're hunting for.

## Before you sign anything

Do a **land search** (carian persendirian or carian rasmi) at the relevant Pejabat Tanah or through the state's e-Tanah portal. It costs very little, takes minutes to days depending on the state, and shows you the current registered owner, the tenure, any charges, and — critically — any sekatan. Never rely on the agent's word or the developer's brochure for this. The title is the truth; everything else is a summary.
